Attributed findings are real race analyses where Off The Pace runs the seven-term lap decomposition against actual historical grands prix and produces precise, verifiable answers to questions that broadcast commentary leaves unanswered. Every number in an attributed finding comes from the same `fct_lap_residuals` pipeline that runs across all 168 races in the 2018–2024 dataset, and every finding must pass the CI-enforced additive identity before it is published.

## What an attributed finding is

The seven-term decomposition separates each lap time into fuel load, tyre compound age, rubber state, ambient conditions, constructor pace, dirty-air penalty, and driver skill residual. On any single race weekend, those seven terms tell you exactly what a driver contributed versus what the car, the tyres, and the strategy handed them.

An attributed finding applies that decomposition to a specific race, isolates the key strategic or competitive moment, and reports the result in seconds not adjectives. The methodology behind every term is documented in the [Seven-Term Identity](/decomposition/seven-term-identity) reference; the findings here are where that methodology meets real race outcomes.

## Why these matter

Lap time alone is a blunt instrument. Two drivers can post identical lap times for completely different reasons: one fighting degraded tyres while the other cruises on fresh rubber, one extracting every tenth in clean air while the other loses pace in a dirty wake. Without decomposition, a commentator watching those numbers scroll by sees parity. The model sees the opposing forces that cancel each other out and makes them visible.

Attributed findings demonstrate that the framework produces **meaningful, non-trivial insights on actual race data** not a theoretical construction that looks clean in a notebook but dissolves when applied to the noise of a real grand prix. If the decomposition can cleanly separate strategy advantage from driver execution at São Paulo 2021, it can do the same across every race in the dataset.

## The primary case study: Hamilton vs Verstappen, São Paulo 2021

The São Paulo 2021 Brazilian Grand Prix is the first and flagship attributed finding. Lewis Hamilton started tenth after a grid penalty and won by 10.5 seconds. Commentary called it brilliant driving. The decomposition finds the precise split.

The key finding: Mercedes' third pit stop on lap 44 manufactured a **2.91 s structural tyre advantage** by the final lap. On the overtake lap itself (lap 59), the tyre age delta contributed **0.36 s** while Hamilton's driver skill residual contributed **1.60 s** strategy and execution in roughly equal measure on the pass that decided the race.

## What makes a good attributed finding

The most revealing attributed findings share a common structure: broadcast commentary produced a confident but vague claim "brilliant driving," "strategy win," "dominant pace" and the decomposition replaces that vagueness with a number. São Paulo 2021 is a clear example: "brilliant driving" is true, but it is also incomplete. The driver contributed 1.60 s on the overtake lap; the strategy contributed 0.36 s; the dirty-air penalty Hamilton absorbed while following Verstappen cost him 6.5 s across the first 14 laps of the final stint. All three of those are invisible in a raw lap time chart.

A finding earns its place here when the decomposition answers a question that the raw data actively obscures where apparent equivalence hides opposing forces, or where a dominant margin turns out to have a more nuanced origin than the headline suggests.
